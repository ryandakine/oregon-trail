WHITFIELD ARRIVES WITH 3 OF 5 AFTER HARROWING JOURNEY
From our correspondent on the Oregon Trail

The wagon of James Whitfield rolled into Oregon City on the morning of October 12th, bearing three weary survivors of what began as a party of five. The journey from Independence, Missouri had taken nearly six months and covered some 1,764 miles of unforgiving terrain.

Whitfield, a carpenter by trade, departed Independence on April 15th with high spirits and a well-provisioned wagon. His party included his wife Mary, their eldest son Thomas, daughter Sarah, and hired hand William Burke. The first weeks passed without serious incident, though the spring rains turned the prairie into a morass that slowed progress to a crawl.

Tragedy first struck near Ash Hollow, where William contracted cholera from contaminated water. Despite Mary's careful nursing and what medicine they carried, the young man succumbed within four days. They buried him beneath a cottonwood tree and pressed on.

The second blow came in the high desert beyond South Pass. Sarah, weakened by short rations and the relentless dust, developed a fever that worsened over three days. She passed quietly in her mother's arms on the morning of September 2nd, within sight of the Snake River.

The remaining Whitfields pushed through the Blue Mountains with grim determination, arriving in the Willamette Valley with barely twenty pounds of flour and two exhausted oxen. "We made it," Whitfield told this reporter. "That's all I can say. We made it."
